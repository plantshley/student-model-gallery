Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis
Dimension Weight CS Score CS Justification BF Score BF Justification
Technical Effectiveness 0.35 4/5 At 90–99% removal, consistently brings receptor concentrations below the WHO odor guideline. Performance is reliable year-round. Main failure mode is loss of alkaline conditions if chemical supply is interrupted or pH drops. 3/5 Same efficiency range on paper (80–99%), but only 84% during the startup period (days 1–9, Alinezhad et al., 2019, Table 1). After startup, performance is sensitive to EBRT, bed moisture, and temperature.
Cost & Practicality 0.30 2/5 Total annualized cost €112,784/year, driven by continuous NaOCl/NaOH consumption (AOC = €112,714/year). Spent scrubbing liquid requires disposal as a waste stream (Alinezhad et al., 2019, Table 6). 4/5 Total annualized cost €29,908/year, with operating costs limited to fan energy and periodic media replacement. No chemical purchases required (Alinezhad et al., 2019, Table 6).
Public Health & Safety 0.20 3/5 Reliably reduces receptor concentrations below the WHO odor guideline with no startup lag. On-site storage of NaOCl and NaOH introduces worker exposure risk during chemical handling and replenishment. 4/5 No hazardous chemicals on site, reducing worker risk. The startup period (84% efficiency, days 1-9) means nearby residents remain exposed above guideline levels during commissioning.
Environmental Considerations 0.20 2/5 H₂S is absorbed into the scrubbing liquid and reacts with NaOCl/NaOH, generating a spent liquid waste stream containing sulfates and chlorine byproducts. Pollution is transferred from air to water rather than destroyed. Continuous chemical replenishment also carries upstream manufacturing and transport emissions. 4/5 H₂S is biologically oxidized to sulfate within the bed media, with no liquid waste stream generated. Lower energy demand than the scrubber since no chemical dosing or pH control systems are required. Spent bed media requires periodic replacement, but is inert compared to chemical waste.
