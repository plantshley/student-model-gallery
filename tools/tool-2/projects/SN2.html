⚙️ Part 3: The Tool

Tool Description: This tool models and compares ground-level NOx concentrations from diesel excavator operations at the Glover Building remodel site at Colorado State University under three scenarios: No Action, Alternative 1 (stack retrofit to 3 m), and Alternative 2 (full fleet electrification). It requires user inputs including emission rate per machine, wind speed, number of machines, atmospheric stability class, and stack heights for each applicable scenario. A simplified Gaussian dispersion model is used because the site represents an outdoor point source with a defined population at a known downwind distance, making this approach appropriate for estimating near ground-level concentrations under varying atmospheric conditions. The tool produces a concentration-versus-distance plot, a bar chart of peak NOx at key receptor distances, a weighted MCDA scoring matrix across four criteria, and a cost estimate comparison with practicality assessment. Users should interpret the concentration outputs as a model to show the risks they are posed to from the diesel excavators when nearby rather than precise predictions, and use the MCDA matrix to understand how technical performance, cost, public health, and environmental considerations together inform which alternative is most defensible given real-world constraints.

✍️ Part 4: Reflection

🏆 Which design choice would you defend, and why?

Based on the tool results, I would choose Alternative 2 to implement within this project. Although full electrification is expensive, it is an investment that provides lasting health benefits for the surrounding public, workers, and the environment. The Gaussian dispersion model showed that the stack retrofit at 3 m produces only modest reductions in ground-level NOx, weakening the case for Alt 1 as an effective solution. Electrification eliminates NOx at the source entirely, making its effectiveness independent of wind speed, atmospheric conditions, or stability class, unlike the stack retrofit, which performs worse under stable low-wind conditions. Additionally, local and federal government incentives for zero-emission equipment may help offset the large upfront investment. While Alternative 2 scored lowest on cost in the MCDA, it scored highest overall when technical effectiveness, public health, and environmental considerations were factored in, making it the most defensible choice given the site's proximity to an occupied academic campus.

⚠️ Where does the comparison break down? What would increase confidence?

The comparison breaks down where model assumptions diverge from real-world conditions. The Gaussian dispersion model requires flat terrain, steady-state wind, constant emission rates, and open rural conditions. None of these accurately describe the Glover Building site, which is surrounded by buildings that create turbulence the Pasquill-Gifford curves are not valid for near structures. The model also treats NOx as an inert tracer, overlooking its contribution to ground-level ozone formation in the Fort Collins area. Wind speed at stack height can differ from ground-level measurements, and emission rates vary with temperature, engine tier, and load factor, all of which were estimated rather than measured. Stability class is also assumed, directly affecting the σy and σz coefficients. To increase confidence, on-site meteorological monitoring, verified engine specifications, and real-time air quality measurements would substantially improve model accuracy.

🤖 How did AI help, and where did you intervene, revise, or override it?

AI served as a collaborator throughout this project rather than simply a service provider. AI initially suggested a 5 m exhaust stack retrofit, but after considering how that system would physically work on a mobile excavator, I determined it would not be practical to stabilize at that height and revised it to 3 m. Seeing how little the 3 m height changed the ground-level NOx concentration compared to No Action was surprising and reinforced why electrification is the stronger alternative. A modest increase in stack height produces only a small change in the Gaussian equation's exponential term, so meaningful reductions require impractically tall stacks. AI also suggested initial MCDA weights, but I set them based on what I thought would matter most to the university and construction company, weighting cost highest at 40% since no alternative can be implemented if it is not financially feasible. The tool could mislead someone into using the concentration outputs to make direct health recommendations, which would be inappropriate given that it is not designed to produce precise predictions. Rather, it is intended to support better engineering decision-making by showing how different design choices affect exposure for the surrounding public and environment under comparable modeled conditions.
