The Tool

Tool Description

This tool estimates uncontrolled and controlled PM2.5 and PM10 emission rates from two power plant alternatives — a pulverized coal (dry-bottom) plant and a natural gas combined-cycle (CCGT) plant — sized to meet a user-specified AI data center electrical demand. For the coal plant, three optional particulate control configurations are available: ESP only, baghouse only, or ESP followed by baghouse in series.

The user sets data center power demand (MW) and coal ash content (%), with thermal efficiencies fixed from EIA literature. EPA AP-42 emission factors yield uncontrolled mass emission rates for PM10 and PM2.5 in lb/hr and g/s. Control efficiencies are user-adjustable within EPA-sourced bounds. When ESP is active, the required collection plate area is calculated via the Deutsch-Anderson equation using a fixed drift velocity of 0.04 m/s for coal fly ash. For series operation, combined efficiency follows η = 1 − (1−η_ESP)(1−η_BH).

Outputs include side-by-side uncontrolled and controlled emission tables, percent reduction, ESP plate area (m²), and a proportional bar chart. Coal-to-gas ratios are shown both before and after controls, revealing how much the emissions gap narrows with control technology. Natural gas requires no particulate controls — all NG PM is below 1 µm and already orders of magnitude lower than uncontrolled coal. All outputs are stack-level mass emission rates, not ambient concentrations.

Outputs are compared against EPA NAAQS standards. Model assumes flat terrain, steady-state meteorology, and a single stability class. Practicality Assessment
⬛ Coal Plant — Implementation Constraints
Permitting timeline (2–5 years)New Source Review under CAA §165 requires Best Available Control Technology (BACT) analysis and public comment. Most likely to prevent timely implementation given data center demand urgency.
Rail & fuel supply infrastructureContinuous coal delivery requires dedicated rail spur or barge access. Site must accommodate multi-day coal storage (~30–60 days supply). Significant if site is remote.
Water supply for coolingPC steam cycle requires large once-through or recirculating cooling. Flat terrain sites may lack adequate water access — requires water rights permitting.
Fly ash disposalBaghouse and ESP generate significant fly ash volume requiring on-site impoundment or off-site disposal. EPA Coal Combustion Residuals (CCR) rule adds regulatory burden.
🔵 Natural Gas CCGT — Implementation Constraints
Pipeline access & capacityCCGT requires firm gas supply contract and pipeline interconnect. If no existing pipeline is adjacent, lateral extension costs can be $1–5M/mile. Most likely single constraint to prevent implementation.
Fuel price exposureUnlike coal (stable long-term contracts), gas prices fluctuate with market. Data center operators need fuel cost certainty for long-term budgeting — financial risk, not technical.
NOx permitting in non-attainment areasIf the project site falls within a NOx non-attainment area, CCGT permitting may require offsets or SCR installation, adding $20–50M to capital cost and 6–18 months to timeline.
Water for cooling (minor)CCGT cycle still requires cooling water, though significantly less than a coal steam cycle. Air-cooled condensers are an option that eliminates water dependency at cost of ~3–5% efficiency penalty.

Scoring methodology: Technical Effectiveness — fully quantitative. Score = 10 × (1 − Q_PM10_alt / Q_PM10_worst), where Q_worst = Coal Uncontrolled. Higher PM10 reduction → higher score. Public Health & Safety — hybrid. 60% weight: PM2.5 emission rate normalized same as Technical Effectiveness. 40% weight: qualitative score (1–10) for secondary risks (fly ash toxicity, NOx/CO co-exposure, coal combustion byproducts), hardcoded per engineering judgment. Environmental Health — hybrid. 50% weight: total PM (PM10 + PM2.5) emission rate normalized. 50% weight: qualitative score (1–10) for solid waste generation, water use, and ecosystem effects. Cost — fully quantitative. Score = 10 × (1 − (C_alt − C_min) / (C_max − C_min)), using 20-year lifecycle cost (low estimate). Natural Gas is cheapest; Coal + ESP→Baghouse is most expensive. Reflection

~300 words. Make your judgment visible.

Which design choice would you defend, and why?
Based on the results of the tool, the best possible design for the power plant to power the data center is a natural gas combined cycle plant. This design is chosen because it is the most environmentally friendly, cheapest, and most technically efficient power generator. The natural gas power plant has low emissions of PM10 and PM2.5 due to the low nucleation of matter of the combusted fuel. As a result, the power plant produces pollutants less than one micron in diameter. Furthermore, the pollutants it does produce are still far less than coal with reasonable control technologies. Coal, on the other hand, was a huge emitter and was extremely costly with the addition of the control technologies. I cannot, in good confidence, recommend coal due to its environmental risks and its huge cost.
Where does your comparison break down? What would you need to know to be more confident?
A potential area that limits the confidence of the results is how temperature affects the control technologies and the potential damages to control technologies. With high temperatures, the fabric of the baghouse can be damaged or rendered useless. Temperatures can also affect the ability of the ESP to collect particulate matter by reducing the amount of electrostatic interactions with the plate. In order to be more confident, I'd need to model temperature based on the amount of fuel combusted. The effects of temperature would affect my MCDA scoring for the Technical Effectiveness by decreasing their scores due to the potential damages, maintenance, and cost. Another factor that limits my confidence in the results is that I assumed that the conditions in the power plant were ideal during the combustion phase. I did not consider power plant startup or shutdown which could have increased the amount of PM due to inefficient combustion. Inefficient combustion would lead to greater environmental and public health risks. This would considerably affect my MCDA scores by reducing the scores for those categories.
How did AI help you, and where did you intervene, revise, or override it?
AI was used differently in this tool than it was in the last tool. I used AI as a collaborator for this project rather than a one-off tool designer. I spent many hours discussing with Claude over design ideas and vision of what I wanted to accomplish for this tool. I discussed with Claude in phases where each phase was a functional unit of the tool. I made sure to ask Claude for sources it used as well as clarifying questions towards specific design aspects. One example of this was when we discussed natural gas emissions of PM 2.5. Claude determined that we shouldn't include PM of those scales because it claimed natural gas didn't produce PM 2.5. While this is accurate for ideal NG combustion, it still makes particulate matter less than 1 micron. For comparison's sake, I wanted NG pollutants to be in a form of total PM. The total amount of PM generated by NG was estimated through emission factors via the AP-42 Section 1.4 where 0.0075 lb/MMBtu of PM is produced in combustion. This was applied to both PM10 and PM 2.5 to achieve a comparable result to the coal combustion calculations. The approach of using Claude as a collaborator turned my ideas into a more refined product. Being able to discuss and challenge Claude was fun and allowed me to better understand AI as a work companion. I also believe working in phases helped Claude and me produce a more comprehensive and informative tool. Doing this reduced the number of iterations and increased the number of times I was able to ask Claude questions before being timed out. Gemini was also used to enhance my flowchart and it made me an image of an AI data center with an on-site power plant.
