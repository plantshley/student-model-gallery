Scenario Annotation
Observation 1
The neighborhood behind the gas station is within the 300m buffer zone. From East to west that affects 9th Street to 5th Street. South of the gas station affects Walnut Street and Elm Street. North of the gas station is commercial buildings, restaurants, and a fire department.
Observation 2
This gas station has eight pumps and is in a high traffic area.
Inference 1
There are two stages in recovery systems. Stage 1 is during the delivery of the fuel into the storage tanks and Stage 2 is during the time of pumping fuel into a vehicle. Regarding the exposure for the people who live within the buffer zone, Stage 1 of the recovery system affects them most. This is due to the short term-high dose exposure during the refilling of the storage tank process, making it a more significant point source emission.
Inference 2
Colorado mandates that gas stations must undergo inspections and refueling employees are trained on safe practices during Stage 1. Gas stations must have vapor tight connections for all fill ports and vapor return lines must use gasket seals to ensure a leak-free connection. Based on these minimum requirements, and that this gas station is in Weld County (a more conservative county), I infer that this gas station has a Coaxial Recovery System.
Explicit Unknown
The fuel delivery schedule for this gas station is unknown. If fuel was delivered once a week, the exposure would be much less than if fuel was delivered five times a week. It would also matter what time of day fuel is delivered because air temperature affects the volume and rate of gas vapors emitted.
Engineering Question
Given that a neighborhood is in close proximity to a high-traffic gas station with eight pumps, would it be more feasible for the gas station to upgrade its Stage 1 recovery system from a Coaxial System to a Dual Point System — or for the residents in the 300-meter buffer zone to invest in a HealthMate Plus Austin Air System that removes VOCs such as benzene?

Overview

Tool Description

This tool is a decision-support instrument comparing two benzene emissions reduction strategies at a gasoline retail station in Windsor, CO. It uses a Gaussian plume dispersion framework with Pasquill–Gifford stability classes to model outdoor benzene concentrations across a 300 m buffer zone, and a single-zone well-mixed indoor model for the air purification alternative. A Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) compares both alternatives across four dimensions: technical effectiveness, cost and practicality, public health and safety, and environmental considerations.

Adjust the input parameters in the Coaxial → Dual Point and HealthMate Plus tabs to explore how atmospheric conditions, emission rates, and receptor distances affect model outputs. The MCDA scores for technical effectiveness update dynamically based on the selected receptor distance. The header chip reflects the currently recommended alternative based on the weighted MCDA total.

This tool was developed with AI collaboration (Claude, Anthropic) for code generation, model structuring, and visualization design. Human judgment was applied to all parameter selection, scenario design, evaluation criteria weighting, and interpretation of results.

Alt 1 — Source Control

Coaxial → Dual Point Vapor Recovery

Coaxial Stage II vapor recovery systems capture approximately 95% of benzene vapors during vehicle refueling. Upgrading to a dual point system increases capture efficiency to approximately 98%, reducing fugitive benzene emissions from 5% to 2% of total vapor volume — a 60% source reduction per refueling event.

This is a source-side intervention: it reduces ambient outdoor concentration for all receptor populations simultaneously — station workers, pedestrians, cyclists, and residential neighbors within the 300 m buffer zone.

Alt 2 — Receptor Control

Austin Air HealthMate Plus Purifier

The Austin Air HealthMate Plus uses a HEPA + activated carbon filtration system rated at 250 CFM CADR for VOCs including benzene. Deployed in the combined living room and kitchen of buffer-zone residences (fixed at 120 m³), it delivers approximately 3.5 ACH from the purifier on top of 0.5 ACH baseline natural ventilation.

This is a receptor-side intervention: it reduces indoor benzene for residents within buffer-zone households only. It provides no reduction to outdoor ambient levels and offers no protection to non-residential populations.

Outdoor Model

Gaussian Plume Dispersion

C(x) = Q / (π · σy · σz · u) · exp(−½ · (H/σz)²)
σy = a·x·(1+0.0001x)^−½
σz = b·x·(1+0.0001x)^−½
Classes B/D/F → a = 0.32 / 0.22 / 0.12
Classes B/D/F → b = 0.24 / 0.16 / 0.08
Indoor Model

Single-Zone Well-Mixed Box

C_in = C_out · F_inf / (1 + ACH_purif / ACH_base)
F_inf = 0.30   ACH_purif = CADR · 3600 / V_room
V_room = 120 m³ (fixed — living + kitchen)
Environmental Modifiers (from Tool 1)

Emission Rate Adjustments

  • Temperature: ×(1 + 0.015·(T−15)) — warmer temps increase benzene volatilization
  • Precipitation: suppresses evaporative emissions (0 = none, 1 = full suppression)
  • Time of day: morning/evening rush ×1.4, midday ×1.0, night ×0.5
  • Breathing rate: adult 7.5 L/min, child 5.0 L/min
  • Safety factor: ×1.5 applied to all concentration estimates
Regulatory Thresholds

Compliance Standards

  • OSHA PEL: 1 ppm (8-hr TWA) — permissible limit for workers
  • NIOSH REL: 0.1 ppm — recommended, more protective standard
  • TWA: C × min(duration, 8) / 8
  • Inhaled dose: C_mg/m³ × breathing rate × duration (in μg)

Technical Effectiveness
How much does each alternative reduce the target pollutant?
Alt 1 — Dual Point System
The dual point system reduces fugitive benzene at the source from 5% to 2% of total vapor volume — a 60% reduction in unrecovered emissions — resulting in measurably lower outdoor concentrations across the entire 300 m buffer zone under all atmospheric conditions modeled. Under stable nighttime conditions (Class F) and low wind, the difference between coaxial and dual point is most pronounced due to reduced atmospheric mixing.
Alt 2 — HealthMate Plus
The HealthMate Plus reduces indoor benzene concentration by approximately 74% within the fixed 120 m³ common-area volume at all receptor distances, as purifier ACH (3.5) substantially exceeds natural ventilation (0.5 ACH). However, this benefit is exclusively for indoor residents and has no measurable effect on ambient outdoor concentrations for any other population.
Cost & Practicality
What does implementation require in money, time, infrastructure, or behavior change?
Alt 1 — Dual Point System
Upgrading from coaxial to dual point vapor recovery requires an estimated $8,000–$15,000 in capital costs per station, regulatory coordination with the Colorado APCD, and a temporary service shutdown for installation — a significant one-time infrastructure investment that requires no ongoing behavioral change from operators or customers.
Alt 2 — HealthMate Plus
The HealthMate Plus costs approximately $700 per unit with no installation requirements beyond placement and power connection. However, its effectiveness depends on continuous household adoption, voluntary resident participation, and recurring filter replacement (~$150 every 3–5 years), creating ongoing financial and behavioral demands across potentially dozens of independent households.
Public Health & Safety
Who is exposed? How does each alternative change exposure for the most affected people?
Alt 1 — Dual Point System
Alt 1 simultaneously reduces ambient benzene for all receptor populations — station workers, pedestrians, cyclists, and residential neighbors — providing the broadest public health benefit. Children, who have higher sensitivity per unit body weight, benefit equally alongside adults from the source-level reduction regardless of their location within the buffer zone.
Alt 2 — HealthMate Plus
Alt 2 protects only indoor residents within buffer-zone households who own and actively operate the purifier, leaving all outdoor exposure unchanged. Children playing outdoors, school bus stops, and non-resident pedestrians receive no protection, and no benefit extends to any populations beyond the immediate household footprint.
Environmental Considerations
Does reducing one pollutant shift another? What are the broader environmental tradeoffs?
Alt 1 — Dual Point System
The dual point system captures benzene vapors before atmospheric release, producing a net reduction in VOC loading to the local airshed with no secondary energy penalty or waste stream. Captured vapors are returned directly to the fuel tank rather than vented, requiring no additional processing or disposal.
Alt 2 — HealthMate Plus
The HealthMate Plus consumes continuous electricity (~125 W at high speed) and generates spent activated carbon filters requiring periodic disposal. Critically, it does not reduce the outdoor benzene source load — the same quantity of benzene continues to be released to the airshed; only its indoor receptor-level impact is mitigated.

Reflection
1 — Which design choice would you defend, and why?
I would defend the first alternative, converting 7-Eleven's Coaxial recovery system to a Dual Point recovery system. The MCDA rated this alternative higher (score of 4.25) than the air purifier alternative (score of 3.8). It outranked the second alternative in all MCDA sectors but one, cost. Coaxial systems become less durable over time and maintenance is inevitable. The owner of the gas station can save several thousand dollars in maintenance costs in the reoccurring years. In addition, when converting from Coaxial to Dual Point, overall emissions of benzene are lower and you remove the risk of vapor escaping into the atmosphere from “burping” the cap. The Dual Point system ensures that the tank is 100% sealed the moment the driver disconnects. After twenty houses purchasing an air filter, that would be about the cost of converting 7-Eleven's recovery system. The houses most at risk would be within the 50–100 meter range with an exposure of about 0.0002 ppm. Given that this is a high traffic area, with plenty of pedestrians from adults walking downtown Windsor to children walking to school, it would benefit the community for this gas station to invest in cleaner recovery systems.
2 — Where does your comparison break down? What would you need to know to be more confident?
What I would need to know to be more confident is to take emission samples from the point source and train my model to reflect more realistic numbers. This is where my uncertainty stems from — perhaps the benzene estimates from my model are overly conservative. If that's the case then most likely no action is needed. If the latter is true, and benzene is being emitted at a higher rate, then adopting a new recovery system is needed. With the estimates the model is outputting now, no action is needed since most values are coming in lower than the OSHA (1 ppm) and NIOSH (0.1 ppm) guidelines.
3 — How did AI help you, and where did you intervene, revise, or override it?
AI did the bulk of the work, creating the Python script that drives the HTML, structuring the information in the HTML, performing the benzene concentration estimate, and even catching that I should not be using a Gaussian Dispersion approach for my air purifier model — and instead should be using a Single Well-Mixed Box approach. Where I came in was reformatting it so that the tool follows the prompt and rubric given in class. It was difficult diving into this assignment without doing some preliminary work such as understanding the difference in Stage 1 and Stage 2 recovery systems, and which would affect the neighboring houses more. AI made it so that the user can answer certain prompts (such as the questions in Part 1 and Part 2), and I had to revise those portions so that the only aspects the user can edit were the inputs from Part 3.
