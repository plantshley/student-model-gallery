Part 1
Embodied Noticing
Emissions Scenario
Pulse-jet baghouse filter at an industrial cement facility. The large inlet and outlet ductwork, external filter housing, and hopper discharge ports at the base are clearly visible. This represents a very common PM filter for many cement kilns.
Observations, Inferences & Engineering Question
Direct Observation 1
This cement facility employs a traditional pulse-jet baghouse where dust-laden exhaust gas is drawn upward through fabric filter bags, with cleaned air discharged out through the top stack.
Direct Observation 2
High-volume cement kiln exhaust enters the filter housing where particulate matter is separated from the gas stream and collected in the hopper assemblies at the base, which are emptied during routine bag maintenance cycles.
Inference 1
The hopper arrangement at the base suggests collected dust is being discharged regularly, implying significant particulate loading in the exhaust stream — typical of clinker and raw meal processing in Portland cement production.
Inference 2
The external ductwork routing suggests the baghouse is installed downstream of the kiln, meaning it is handling hot, potentially moisture-laden gas, which would stress filter media over time and drive up maintenance frequency. It is likely that the entering volume has been cooled but may still be hot enough for the bag material to be of concern when filtering PM.
Explicit Unknown
The current filtration efficiency, bag material, and pressure drop across this specific unit are unknown — all of which are important to understand the effectiveness of the filter which only the external housing is visible for.
Engineering Question
For a cement kiln with elevated temperature and high particulate exhaust, which control configuration — a standalone baghouse like the one depicted, a standalone Electrostatic Precipitator (ESP), or a hybrid ESP-baghouse system — will provide the best balance of PM removal efficiency, operational reliability, and lifecycle cost?

Tool Description
This tool models ground-level particulate matter (PM) concentrations downwind of a cement kiln using a simplified Gaussian dispersion approach, comparing three engineering control alternatives against an uncontrolled baseline. The user adjusts four inputs — stack height (H), wind speed (u), atmospheric stability class (A–F), and which alternatives to display — and the tool outputs a concentration vs. distance profile from 100 m to 10 km, peak concentration, receptor-point concentration at 1,500 m, and the downwind distance at which each alternative achieves EPA NAAQS PM₂.₅ compliance (9 µg/m³). The Gaussian model was selected because the cement kiln represents a continuous elevated point source in open terrain, well-suited to the Pasquill-Gifford dispersion framework. The only variable that changes between alternatives is the emission rate Q (g/s), which applies each device's rated removal efficiency to the uncontrolled kiln emission of 75 g/s. Outputs represent worst-case centerline ground-level concentrations under steady-state conditions — real concentrations will vary with wind direction, terrain, and equipment aging.
