Scenario Overview

Strang Pit · SE Fort Collins
Strang Pit · Larimer County Roads & Bridge · Horsetooth Rd & Strauss Cabin Rd, Fort Collins, CO · Source: Larimer County (2025)
Source

The Strang Pit is a 160-acre, county-owned gravel quarry near Horsetooth Rd & Strauss Cabin Rd in southeast Fort Collins, operated by Larimer County Roads & Bridge since 1977. It produces ~40,000 tons/yr of aggregate for unpaved road maintenance. The primary air quality concern is fugitive PM10 dust from haul roads and active stockpile faces.

Engineering Alternatives
ALT A — WATER SUPPRESSION
Periodic water truck application on unpaved haul roads and active stockpile faces. Standard practice. Control efficiency: ~50% PM10 reduction (EPA AP-42).
ALT B — CHEMICAL DUST STABILIZER
Lignosulfonate applied to haul roads, binding surface particles. Less frequent reapplication than water. Control efficiency: ~85% PM10 reduction (EPA AP-42).
Receptor Communities
RECEPTOR 1 · Kechter Farm Neighborhood · ~1,500 m SE
Residential neighborhood southeast of the pit. Primary human health receptor. Downwind on prevailing NW→SE winds.
RECEPTOR 2 · Fossil Creek Reservoir Natural Area · ~3,000 m SE
Popular natural area with trails and wildlife. Environmental receptor. Further downwind — captures longer-range transport.

OBSERVATIONS — What is visible / known
OBS 1

Unpaved haul roads and exposed aggregate stockpile faces are visible across the 160-acre site. Vehicle traffic on these surfaces generates visible fugitive dust plumes consistent with EPA AP-42 §11.19.2 emission factors for aggregate quarries (~0.6 kg PM10 per vehicle pass).

OBS 2

The Kechter Farm residential neighborhood (~1,500 m SE) lies directly downwind of the pit along the dominant NW→SE wind corridor recorded at Fort Collins weather stations. The quarry has operated continuously since 1977 with no documented emission controls beyond routine county practice.

INFERENCES — What may be occurring
INF 1

During dry summer months and afternoon unstable atmospheric conditions (Pasquill-Gifford Class C–D), PM10 concentrations at Kechter Farm likely approach or exceed the 24-hr NAAQS standard of 150 µg/m³ — an exceedance risk invisible to residents without dispersion modeling.

INF 2

The county's current practice likely relies on ad hoc water application (if any), which is insufficient during dry spells. A chemical stabilizer such as lignosulfonate — applied less frequently but with ~85% PM10 control efficiency — would offer meaningfully better protection without a proportional increase in operational cost.

EXPLICIT UNKNOWN
UNK

The actual daily vehicle trip count on haul roads is not publicly documented. This model uses an estimated 20–60 trips/day based on a 40,000 ton/yr production rate and typical haul truck capacity (~20 tons/load). Real trip frequency could be higher during peak season, making the uncontrolled emission rate a significant source of uncertainty in all concentration estimates.

ENGINEERING QUESTION

"Between periodic water suppression and lignosulfonate chemical stabilization, which haul-road dust control strategy most effectively reduces PM10 concentrations at the Kechter Farm receptor to below the 24-hr NAAQS threshold — and under what operating conditions does each approach fail?"

Part 3 — The Tool

19 pts
What the tool does. This tool is a web-based decision-support instrument that uses a simplified Gaussian plume dispersion model to compare two dust control alternatives — periodic water suppression (Alt A) and lignosulfonate chemical stabilization (Alt B) — for fugitive PM10 emissions from the Strang Pit aggregate quarry in southeast Fort Collins. It accepts user-adjustable inputs including annual production rate, operating schedule, EPA AP-42 emission factors for haul roads and stockpiles, control efficiencies for each alternative, mean wind speed, and Pasquill-Gifford atmospheric stability class. The tool implements the ground-level Gaussian plume equation C(x) = Q / (π · σy · σz · u) with Pasquill-Gifford dispersion coefficients, computing PM10 concentrations at 13 downwind distances for all three scenarios (baseline, Alt A, Alt B) and reporting results at two named receptor locations: Kechter Farm (~1,500 m SE) and Fossil Creek Reservoir Natural Area (~3,000 m SE). Output includes an interactive concentration-vs-distance chart, a receptor summary table with percent reduction values, a sensitivity heatmap sweeping wind speed and stability class, and a multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) panel that auto-scores technical effectiveness from model output and allows the user to adjust qualitative scores and criterion weights for cost & practicality, public health & safety, and environmental considerations, producing a weighted recommendation. To interpret results: concentration values at the receptor cards represent worst-case daytime conditions under the selected stability class; values approaching or exceeding the PM10 24-hr NAAQS of 150 µg/m³ indicate a potential compliance concern; the sensitivity heatmap identifies conditions (high stability, low wind) where concentrations are highest and control efficiency matters most; and the MCDA panel translates the quantitative model output into a structured comparative recommendation that accounts for non-technical factors.

Part 4 — Reflection

5 pts
Design Recommendation — Which alternative and why

I would defend Alt B — lignosulfonate chemical stabilization as the preferred dust control strategy for Strang Pit. Under default model conditions (neutral stability, 4 m/s wind, 40,000 tons/yr), Alt B reduces PM10 at Kechter Farm by approximately 85% compared to 50% for Alt A, bringing modeled concentrations well below the 24-hr NAAQS threshold across all tested wind and stability conditions. The technical advantage is not marginal — Alt B produces roughly a 2.3× greater absolute concentration reduction at the primary residential receptor. Beyond the modeling: lignosulfonate requires reapplication only monthly or after rain events, whereas water suppression demands daily truck operations, fuel, and driver labor that compound over a Colorado dry season. The higher upfront material cost of the chemical stabilizer is offset by substantially lower recurring operational burden, and its binding mechanism is not compromised by the high-temperature, low-humidity summer days when dust risk is greatest and water evaporation undermines the suppression benefit. For a county-operated facility with a fixed roads budget, the reliability and consistency of Alt B makes it the more defensible choice for protecting the Kechter Farm neighborhood.

Limitations — Where the comparison breaks down

The most significant limitation is the emission rate uncertainty. This model derives Q from EPA AP-42 §11.19.2 emission factors and an estimated production schedule, but actual haul road emissions depend on vehicle speed, road surface silt content, and trip frequency — none of which are documented in publicly available Strang Pit records. A higher-than-assumed trip count would increase baseline concentrations proportionally and could push even Alt B scenarios above compliance thresholds. Second, the Gaussian plume model assumes a single steady-state wind direction, which overstates concentrations during variable-wind periods but may understate peak exposures during channeled drainage flow events in the evening when the plume tracks directly toward Kechter Farm. Third, the model treats the pit as a point source at its centroid — in reality, haul road emissions are distributed spatially across the 160-acre site, and a line or area source formulation would be more physically accurate. To increase confidence, I would need measured silt content and vehicle count data from the county, an anemometer record at or near the pit to characterize local wind variability, and ideally a PM10 ambient monitoring dataset at Kechter Farm against which to validate the model output under known conditions.

AI Collaboration — How AI helped and where I intervened

AI collaboration was central to building this tool. The initial HTML scaffold — tab structure, CSS design system, chart initialization, and MCDA UI framework — was co-generated with Claude, which I used as a coding collaborator rather than a code generator: I described the architecture I wanted, reviewed each section, and directed revisions to the equation rendering, receptor snap logic in the model table, and the heatmap color scaling function. The sensitivity analysis table structure was AI-generated from a functional specification I wrote. I overrode the AI's initial MCDA scoring formula, which normalized scores as percentages of maximum — I replaced it with a raw weighted-sum approach (score × weight / 100) because I judged the normalized version obscured the actual magnitude of score differences between alternatives. For Part 2, Claude generated the SVG system diagram from a textual description; I revised it to correct the P-G notation, adjust receptor distances to match modeled values, and add the uncertainty/limits callout that the AI omitted. Throughout, my interventions centered on two things: engineering accuracy (checking that equations and units were correct) and interpretive clarity (ensuring outputs communicated decision-relevant information rather than just numbers).
