🛠️ Part 3 – The Tool

Recognized model: simplified Gaussian dispersion Alternatives: flare optimization vs VRU Decision support, not regulatory compliance modeling

This tool compares two emissions-reduction alternatives for a petrochemical VOC source under the same meteorological and source assumptions. The user enters an emission rate Q (g/s), wind speed U (m/s), effective release height H (m), receptor distance range (m), and atmospheric stability class. A simplified Gaussian plume approach is used because the scenario is an outdoor industrial source with downwind transport and a need for a transparent first-pass comparison. The model estimates ground-level centerline concentration (mg/m³) for the base case and then applies two alternatives: flare optimization with an assumed 40% emissions reduction and a vapor recovery unit (VRU) with an assumed 85% reduction. These are representative assumed values based on typical literature ranges and are used for comparative analysis only. The outputs include concentration profiles, a receptor-specific comparison table, a cost table, sensitivity information, and a weighted MCDA. The results should be interpreted as structured engineering evidence for comparing alternatives rather than as a final site-specific compliance prediction.

📘 Gaussian model explanation

The tool uses a simplified ground-level centerline Gaussian plume equation:

C(x) = Q / (π U σy σz) × exp[ -H² / (2σz²) ]

  • Q = emission rate (g/s)
  • U = wind speed (m/s)
  • H = effective release height (m)
  • σy, σz = lateral and vertical dispersion parameters that vary with distance and atmospheric stability
  • C(x) = ground-level centerline concentration (reported here in mg/m³)

This formulation is appropriate for a comparative outdoor screening analysis because it links emissions and meteorology directly to downwind concentration in a transparent way. It does not fully capture complex plant geometry, transient releases, buoyant plume rise, VOC chemistry, terrain effects, or detailed site meteorology. Those limitations matter when moving from early design comparison to final engineering decision-making. This formulation is appropriate for screening-level analysis but should be supplemented with site-specific dispersion modeling (e.g., AERMOD) for regulatory or design decisions.

Engineering note:
The most important meteorological driver in this simplified model is usually wind speed (m/s), because dilution changes directly as U changes. Atmospheric stability also matters because it changes how quickly the plume spreads vertically and laterally.
Field reality note:
Rated or nominal control efficiency is not the same as field performance. Real results depend on operating load, maintenance quality, flare tuning, vapor composition, capture reliability, and how well the technology fits the facility’s process layout.
System design note:
Real industrial control decisions are often made within larger control trains and under rising marginal cost of control. This tool compares two VOC-focused options, but a final plant design would still need broader system-level review.

MCDA justification:
  • Technical effectiveness: VRU achieves higher VOC reduction due to direct vapor capture, while flare optimization improves but does not eliminate emissions.
  • Cost & practicality: Flare optimization is simpler and lower cost; VRU requires higher capital and system integration.
  • Public health & safety: VRU reduces direct exposure more effectively by preventing VOC release.
  • Environmental: VRU avoids combustion byproducts, while flaring may generate secondary pollutants.

📝 Part 4 – Reflection

If I had to defend one design choice for this Jubail industrial case, I would defend the Vapor Recovery Unit (VRU) as the stronger long-term option. The main reason is that it reduces VOC emissions much more aggressively than flare optimization, and that difference appears directly in the modeled downwind concentrations. From an air quality engineering perspective, the important question is not just whether emissions are being managed, but whether concentrations at meaningful receptors—such as worker routes, nearby operational areas, or site boundaries—are reduced enough to matter. A VRU also offers a broader environmental advantage because it captures vapors rather than relying mainly on combustion performance, which better supports resource recovery and lower atmospheric loading.

The comparison still breaks down in important ways. The most influential assumption in this tool is probably wind speed, followed by atmospheric stability and effective release height, because those inputs strongly affect predicted concentration in the Gaussian model. Under low wind conditions, the difference between alternatives becomes more critical, as reduced dispersion leads to higher ground-level concentrations. The tool also assumes steady emissions, simplified dispersion coefficients, inert VOC behavior, and fixed control efficiencies. Real flare performance depends on operating conditions, and real VRU performance depends on vapor composition, capture reliability, maintenance, and process integration. To be more confident, I would want site-specific Jubail meteorological data, better source characterization, actual receptor locations, and vendor or operating data for both control technologies.
